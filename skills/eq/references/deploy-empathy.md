# Deploy Empathy — Michele Hansen

## Posture

Deploy Empathy is officially a customer-interview book, but the underlying skill is "how to get a real person to tell you what's actually going on for them, without you contaminating the answer." Almost all of it transfers directly to personal communication when Zane's job is **to understand, not to persuade**: figuring out why a friend's been distant, what a partner actually needs after a hard week, what's behind a coworker's frustration, what a family member is really asking for under a surface complaint.

The whole posture is **suspended judgement**. You create a bubble where the other person can think out loud without being corrected, sold to, or fixed. The bubble is what produces the real answer — which is almost always different from the first answer.

Use this frame when Zane catches himself wanting to leap to a solution, defend his side, or "help" before he actually knows what's going on.

## The moves

### 1. Use a gentle tone of voice

Not soft or saccharine — neutral, low-stakes, curious. The voice of someone who has nowhere to be. The tone signals "you can take your time, I'm not trying to push you anywhere."

### 2. Validate before clarifying

When someone shares something hard, the first move is not a question or a fix — it's acknowledging that what they feel makes sense given their reality. **You don't have to agree with their conclusion to validate the feeling.** Phrases:

- "That makes sense."
- "Yeah, I can see why that would land that way."
- "Of course you felt that way."

This isn't agreement; it's "I heard you, and you're not crazy for feeling this." Until someone feels that, they can't think clearly with you.

### 3. Leave pauses for them to fill

Silence is not an awkward pause — silence is the sound that thinking and remembering make. Most people fill it within 2–3 seconds because they're uncomfortable. **Don't.** Count to five before saying anything after they trail off. The thing that comes after the pause is almost always more honest than the first answer.

### 4. Mirror their last few words as a question

Lightweight technique: when someone says something with weight, repeat the last 3–5 words back, with an upward inflection.

> Them: "…and I just felt like I was being a burden again."
> You: "A burden again?"

This invites them to keep going on the part that mattered, without you imposing a frame. Use sparingly — once per topic, not as a tic.

### 5. Don't explain, don't negate

Two reflexes to suppress:

- **Don't explain.** When they say something that confuses you or feels off, resist the urge to clarify what you meant or fill them in on context. Stay with their experience.
- **Don't negate.** Don't say "no, that's not what I meant" or "actually it was…" mid-flow. Even gentle corrections shut the bubble. If a correction is needed, save it for later.

### 6. Let them be the expert on their own experience

You don't know what it's like to be them. Even with people you know intimately, assume their inner experience is partly opaque to you. Ask, then take what they say as the data, not as something to verify against your prior.

> "What was that actually like for you?"
> "What's the part that's hardest right now?"
> "If you had to put words on what you're needing here, what would it be?"

### 7. Use their words, not yours

When you reflect back or follow up, use **their** language — the exact nouns, verbs, and metaphors they used. If they said "I felt invisible at the dinner", don't paraphrase to "you felt unseen." Stay literal. Their words are the closest mapping to their actual experience; your synonyms drift.

### 8. Ask about specific past instances, not hypotheticals

Hypotheticals get you fiction. Past tense gets you data. Instead of "what would you want in that situation?" ask "tell me about the last time that happened — what did you do?" People are way better at reporting what happened than predicting what they'd do.

### 9. Anchor to time and money already spent

When you need to understand how much something matters to someone, ask what they've already paid for it — in time, energy, money, attention. "How long have you been thinking about this?" "How many times has it come up between you and them?" These reveal scale better than "how big a deal is this for you?"

## Worked example

**Situation.** A close friend has been canceling plans for two months. Zane is starting to feel rejected and wants to bring it up.

**Wrong move (leads with own story):**
> "Hey, I've noticed you've been canceling a lot, and I'm starting to wonder if something's off between us."

This frames the conversation around Zane's interpretation and asks the friend to either reassure him or defend themselves. Either way, he doesn't learn what's actually going on.

**Deploy Empathy version:**
> "Hey, I wanted to check in — I noticed plans have been getting harder to pin down on your end. *[gentle tone]* No agenda, I just want to understand what's going on for you these days." *[Pause. Wait.]*

> Friend: "Yeah, it's been… I don't know, things have been kind of a lot."
> Zane: *[pause]* "Kind of a lot?" *[mirror]*
> Friend: "Like work-wise and with my mom and I'm just exhausted."
> Zane: "That sounds heavy. *[validate]* What's been the hardest part?" *[their experience as the data]*

The conversation that follows is the real one. Zane's "I'm starting to feel rejected" can come later, after he actually knows what's happening — and probably won't need to be said the same way once he does.

## When this frame is the wrong tool

- **When you need to deliver hard content.** This is a listening frame, not a confronting frame. If Zane has something he needs to say (boundary, request, hard feedback), use Crucial Conversations' STATE instead. Listening alone won't get the message across.
- **When the other person is using the bubble to evade.** Some people will fill an open invitation with vagueness or victim story forever. After two or three real attempts to draw out the substance, switch to direct questions.
- **When you yourself are dysregulated.** Deploy Empathy requires you to have enough space inside you to hold someone else's experience without reacting. If you're flooded, postpone.

## What this book is best at

Listening conversations. Understanding what's underneath a surface complaint. Getting past someone's first (rehearsed, defensive, or socially-acceptable) answer to what they actually think and feel. Helping someone Zane cares about feel genuinely heard.
